# CHAPTER TWO

## LITERATURE REVIEW

This chapter reviews the scholarly and technical literature that informs the design of an AI-enhanced multi-tenant family fund management system. It is organised into five sections: historical perspectives on family fund management, the theoretical framework behind the system, a review of related work, a discussion of gaps in existing research, and a summary tying the literature back to this project. The chapter draws on over fifty sources from fields including informal finance, software architecture, access control, financial technology, machine learning, natural language processing, and web security.

### 2.1 Historical Perspectives of Family Fund Management Systems

#### 2.1.1 Origins of Informal Collective Finance in Africa

Long before any formal bank set up shop on the continent, people across Sub-Saharan Africa were already pooling money in structured, socially governed ways. Ardener (1964) published one of the earliest systematic studies of these arrangements—what she called rotating savings and credit associations, or ROSCAs—and found them in virtually every culture she examined. Her key insight was that these groups were not just financial tools. They were social institutions, held together by trust, reciprocity, and shared obligation. Bouman (1995) reinforced this point with evidence from across the continent, showing that ROSCAs persisted even in places where banks had become accessible.

In Nigeria, these arrangements took on distinctive cultural forms depending on the region. Among the Yoruba in the southwest, the *ajo* system has members contribute a fixed amount on a regular cycle, with each person receiving the total pot once per rotation (Bascom, 1952). The *esusu*, also Yoruba in origin but practised more broadly, works similarly but with tighter structure—some variants appoint a treasurer to oversee collections and disbursements (Akanji, 2006). Up north, Hausa-speaking communities run *adashi* groups, where a pool of contributions either rotates among members or is held collectively for agreed-upon purposes (Iganiga & Asemota, 2008). And across francophone West and Central Africa, the *tontine*—named after the seventeenth-century Italian banker Lorenzo de Tonti—fills much the same role, sometimes blurring the line between savings and micro-lending (Ardener & Burman, 1995).

What holds all these systems together, structurally, is a shared set of features: membership is voluntary, contributions happen on a fixed schedule, the payout follows a predetermined rule, and the whole thing is governed by social norms rather than legal contracts (Bouman, 1995). They survive not because people lack alternatives but because they offer something banks often do not—low barriers to entry, flexibility, minimal fees, and the social pressure that actually makes people follow through on their savings commitments (Aryeetey, 2008; Oloyede, 2008). Gugerty (2007) showed empirically in Kenya that ROSCA participation functioned as a "commitment device," helping members overcome the self-control problems that made saving on their own so difficult. That finding applies just as well in Nigeria, where many people join *ajo* or family funds precisely because having a group hold them accountable works better than relying on willpower alone.

#### 2.1.2 The Family Purse: A Distinct Non-Rotating Collective Fund

ROSCAs like *ajo*, *esusu*, and *adashi* have attracted the bulk of scholarly attention when it comes to informal African finance. But there is a related arrangement that gets far less coverage in the literature: the **family contribution fund**, often called the family purse. The mechanics are fundamentally different. In a ROSCA, the pooled money rotates—each member takes a turn receiving the pot. In a family purse, nothing rotates. Members pay into a **non-rotating, centrally managed treasury**, and the money stays there until it is needed: a funeral, a hospital bill, someone's school fees, a family celebration, repairs to the family house (Oloyede, 2008; Apt, 2002).

Several features set the family purse apart. For one, contributions are often tiered by economic capacity rather than uniform. A working professional might contribute ₦4,000 a month while a student pays ₦1,000—an equity-based differentiation that standard ROSCAs do not typically accommodate. For another, governance is usually centralised in a single person: an elder or someone elected as financial secretary, who collects the money, keeps the records, authorises spending, and reports to the rest of the family (Aryeetey, 2008; Bouman, 1995). This centralisation has obvious efficiency benefits, but it also creates a single point of failure. If the financial secretary makes mistakes—or worse, acts dishonestly—the entire fund's integrity is compromised, and the rest of the family may have no way to verify the records independently.

Quantifying how common family purses are in Nigeria is tricky because they operate informally and do not show up in conventional financial surveys. But the circumstantial evidence is strong. The World Bank Global Findex Database found that about 55% of Nigerian adults who save do so informally rather than through banks (Demirgüç-Kunt et al., 2022). EFInA (2023) estimated that more than 36 million Nigerian adults depend entirely on informal financial groups for their savings and credit needs. Given how central extended family structures are to Nigerian social life (Apt, 2002), it is reasonable to assume that family-level funds make up a substantial portion of that informal activity.

#### 2.1.3 Management Challenges: From Notebooks to Messaging Platforms

For most of their history, family contribution funds have been managed with whatever was at hand—a notebook, a personal ledger, maybe a sheet of paper pinned to a wall. The designated financial secretary would write down who paid, how much, and when, and keep a running tally of what each member owed (Adeyemo & Bamire, 2005). At small scale, this works well enough. But these records are physically fragile. They can be lost, damaged, or altered. They contain no built-in error checking. And when a dispute arises—did Brother Musa actually pay last March?—there is no independent audit trail. The disagreement gets settled through memory and social negotiation, which is not the same thing as verification.

The arrival of smartphones and messaging apps changed the communication side of the problem but did not solve the management side. Many family fund administrators now use WhatsApp groups to announce due dates, confirm they received someone's payment, and share occasional summaries (Aker & Mbiti, 2010). This helps with reach, especially when family members are scattered across different cities or even countries. But a WhatsApp group is not a management system. Messages scroll away and become effectively lost. There is no way to search them structurally, compute balances, or generate reports. A message saying "Received 20k from Brother Musa" tells you nothing about whether that was a full or partial payment, which month it covers, or what balance remains. The ambiguity that manual records created is just replicated in a digital chat format.

These problems are not unique to family funds. Adeyemo and Bamire (2005) and Anderson and Baland (2002) found that manual record-keeping was a leading source of disputes and a primary reason informal financial groups dissolved. Aryeetey (2008) reached similar conclusions in broader studies of informal financial institutions—when record-keeping breaks down, trust follows, and once trust is gone, the group rarely survives.

#### 2.1.4 The Digital Transformation of Cooperative Finance

Africa's fintech landscape has changed dramatically since M-Pesa launched in Kenya in 2007. That platform proved that mobile-phone-based financial services could reach populations that formal banks had never managed to serve—the unbanked and underbanked who make up a large share of the continent's adults (Mbiti & Weil, 2016). In the years since, Nigeria has built its own payment infrastructure through companies like Paystack, Flutterwave, and Interswitch, making digital transactions routine for a growing segment of the population (Aker & Mbiti, 2010; Paystack, 2024).

But here is the thing: this digital revolution has mostly bypassed group-based finance. The platforms that have achieved real scale in Nigeria—PiggyVest for automated savings, Cowrywise for savings and investment—are designed for individuals managing their own money (PiggyVest, 2024; Cowrywise, 2024). They have no concept of a group with shared governance, collective funds, and differentiated member roles. On the cooperative side, CreditClan and Lendsqr provide digital tools for formal lending operations—Lendsqr specifically offers white-label lending infrastructure for fintech companies and digital lenders, including embedded credit scoring and loan lifecycle management (CreditClan, 2024). These are sophisticated platforms, but they serve a completely different use case—registered cooperative societies that must comply with CAMA and CBN regulations. An informal family contribution group has no use for regulatory compliance features and does not fit the assumptions these platforms are built on.

A handful of applications have tried to target group savings more directly. Esusu, for instance, draws on the West African concept in its name but is actually a US-based platform focused on rent reporting for credit building. Various M-Pesa-integrated group savings tools exist in East Africa (Mbiti & Weil, 2016). None of these combine the particular set of features that family fund management demands: multi-tenant data isolation so different families' data never bleeds together, tiered contributions based on member categories, role-based access control distinguishing administrators from financial secretaries from ordinary members, partial payment tracking with automated oldest-balance-first allocation, and integrated reporting. And that is before even considering the predictive analytics and intelligent narrative reporting that this project proposes.

#### 2.1.5 The Imperative for Purpose-Built Solutions

Stepping back, the historical picture is clear on three points. First, collective financial management within families and kinship networks is a deeply rooted practice across Nigeria and the broader region—not a niche activity but a mainstream one, embedded in how millions of people actually manage their money. Second, the family purse model is structurally different from the ROSCAs that dominate the academic literature, and it creates management challenges—tiered contributions, partial payments, centralised and often unverifiable record-keeping—that ROSCAs do not. Third, despite real progress in African fintech, no existing platform specifically targets this use case with the right combination of features, as demonstrated in the comparative analysis presented in Table 1.1. Individual savings apps ignore groups. Cooperative platforms assume regulatory formality. Group savings tools from other markets lack the architectural and functional depth the problem requires.

That gap—between the scale of the practice and the absence of purpose-built digital tools—is what this project sets out to close.

### 2.2 Theoretical Framework

This section sets out the ideas and models behind the system's design. Not every subsection here introduces cutting-edge theory—some of these concepts have been around for decades. But each one matters for a specific reason in the context of building an intelligent family fund platform, and it is worth being explicit about why.

#### 2.2.1 Multi-Tenant Software Architecture

The basic idea behind multi-tenancy is deceptively simple: instead of running a separate copy of the application for every customer (or in this case, every family), you run one instance and let it serve everyone. Each independent group—each *tenant*—gets its own slice of the system, with its data kept separate from everyone else's (Bezemer & Zaidman, 2010). The approach emerged alongside cloud computing and the SaaS model because the alternative—spinning up dedicated infrastructure for each customer—gets expensive fast (Chong et al., 2006).

There are really only three ways to do it. You can give each tenant their own database entirely. Maximum isolation, maximum cost—the *shared-nothing* approach. Or you can have everyone share one database but keep their tables separated into different schemas. Or—the lightest-weight option—you share everything, database and schema included, and use a discriminator column like `tenant_id` to tell one group's rows apart from another's (Chong et al., 2006).

FamilyFunds takes the third path. Every record—contributions, payments, expenses, member accounts—carries a `family_id` that binds it to a particular family. For an application that might eventually host hundreds of small groups, each with maybe fifteen or twenty members, this is the only strategy that makes economic sense. But it comes with a cost of a different kind: the entire burden of keeping Family A's data away from Family B falls on the application code. Every query has to be scoped. Every policy check has to confirm that the person making the request belongs to the family whose data they want to see. Miss even one scoping check, and one family could accidentally—or deliberately—peek into another family's financial records. In a platform built on trust, that would be fatal (Bezemer & Zaidman, 2010).

So the takeaway is this: shared-schema multi-tenancy pushes data isolation out of the database layer and into the application layer. The middleware, the authorisation policies, the query scoping logic—those become just as important as the database design itself.

#### 2.2.2 Role-Based Access Control

Sandhu et al. (1996) formalised what most organisations already knew intuitively: rather than assigning permissions to individuals one by one (which is tedious and error-prone), you define a set of roles and attach permissions to those. Users get whatever permissions their role gives them. NIST later turned this into a reference model with four flavours: basic role-to-permission mapping (Core RBAC), roles that can inherit from other roles (Hierarchical RBAC), and two kinds of separation-of-duty constraints that prevent dangerous combinations of roles (Ferraiolo et al., 2001).

FamilyFunds does not need most of that complexity. Three roles. Administrator: controls settings, manages members, sees everything. Financial Secretary: records payments, logs expenses, generates reports, but cannot mess with family settings or add and remove people. Member: can view their own contribution history and pay online, nothing more. No inheritance, no dynamic role switching. Flat and simple.

But the simplicity is the point. The entire governance problem in family funds boils down to who gets to do what. When Uncle Ahmed is the financial secretary and he tracks everything in a notebook, there is no mechanism stopping him from changing a record or claiming he never received a payment. Nobody else can verify anything. RBAC turns that informal social arrangement—"Ahmed handles the money"—into something the system actually enforces (Sandhu et al., 1996). A member literally cannot alter payment records, no matter how hard they try. That technical guarantee is what separates the platform from a WhatsApp group and a spiral-bound notebook.

#### 2.2.3 Automated Payment Allocation Logic

Here is a problem that sounds trivial until you actually have to solve it. A member owes ₦4,000 each for January, February, and March. She sends ₦6,000. Which months does that cover? January in full (₦4,000), February in part (₦2,000 of the ₦4,000), and March stays untouched. Obvious, right?

It is obvious if you process debts oldest-first—what computer scientists would call a First-In, First-Out queue (Cormen et al., 2009). The system walks through outstanding contributions from the earliest to the most recent, applying available funds at each step until the payment is exhausted or there is nothing left to pay. FamilyFunds codifies this as an oldest-balance-first allocation algorithm.

The reason this matters so much is not algorithmic elegance. It is consistency. Ask three different financial secretaries how they would allocate that ₦6,000, and you might get three different answers. One credits the current month first. Another splits it evenly. A third does not track partial payments at all and just writes "₦6,000 received" next to the member's name. The deterministic FIFO approach eliminates that ambiguity entirely. The same payment in, the same allocation out, every time. Any member can look at their statement and see exactly how their money was distributed. That kind of transparency is a precondition for trust—and trust, as the historical section made clear, is what keeps these funds alive.

#### 2.2.4 Supervised Machine Learning for Payment Prediction

Predicting whether someone will pay their contribution on time or default is, at its core, a binary classification problem. Will they or won't they? This framing puts the project squarely in the territory of credit scoring, which has been one of the most heavily researched applications of machine learning in finance since at least the early 2000s (Thomas, 2000; Khandani et al., 2010).

The mechanics are straightforward. A supervised model trains on historical data where the outcome is already known—months where someone paid and months where they did not—and learns to spot patterns that distinguish the two (Hastie et al., 2009). The algorithms available range from logistic regression, which has the virtue of being simple and interpretable, to more powerful ensemble methods like random forests (Breiman, 2001) and gradient-boosted trees (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) that squeeze out higher accuracy at the cost of transparency and data hunger.

But here is where the family fund context creates a real wrinkle. Banks train credit scoring models on millions of loan records. A family fund with twenty members and a year of history has 240 data points. That is not a dataset—it is a napkin. At that scale, complex models do not learn patterns; they memorise noise. Hastie et al. (2009) are very clear about this: when data is scarce, simpler models with fewer parameters almost always win. A logistic regression that captures two or three meaningful signals—payment history, months since last default, category tier—will likely outperform an XGBoost model that tries to be clever with features that do not generalise.

There is also the evaluation headache. In most families, most members pay on time. If 90% of records are "paid," a model that blindly predicts "paid" every time scores 90% accuracy. That number is useless. Metrics like precision, recall, F1-score, and the area under the ROC curve give a much more honest picture (Hand, 2009; Lessmann et al., 2015). The predictive module has to be designed with both the small-data constraint and the class-imbalance problem baked in from the start, not patched on after the fact.

#### 2.2.5 Natural Language Generation and Large Language Models

The older approach to automatically turning data into readable text—Natural Language Generation, or NLG—followed a pipeline with distinct stages: figure out what information to include, decide how to organise it, choose the words, stitch them into sentences (Reiter & Dale, 2000). Systems built this way powered things like automated weather forecasts and routine sports match summaries. They worked, but they were rigid. Building one for a new domain meant engineering a new pipeline from scratch.

Then Large Language Models arrived and made most of that pipeline irrelevant. GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), both built on the Transformer architecture that Vaswani et al. (2017) introduced, showed that a single neural network trained on enormous quantities of text could generate fluent, contextually aware prose across virtually any domain—no hand-crafted templates needed. You feed it structured data, tell it what kind of output you want, and it writes.

For FamilyFunds, the practical application looks like this: the system compiles a month's financial data—₦52,000 collected against a ₦60,000 target, three members still outstanding, ₦8,000 in expenses—and hands it to the LLM with instructions to produce a narrative summary. What comes back is a paragraph a family elder can actually read and understand, not a table of numbers that requires explanation.

The catch is reliability. LLMs can hallucinate—confidently produce numbers or facts that are simply wrong. In a family financial report, a hallucinated figure could cause real confusion or conflict. The mitigation strategy is to pass all the data in the prompt itself, so the model is translating structured inputs into natural language rather than generating information from memory. That constraint, combined with clear formatting instructions, keeps the output grounded. But the cost per API call, the latency of generation, and the dependency on an external service are all trade-offs that the system's subscription tiers have to account for.

#### 2.2.6 Agile Development Methodology

Software development methodology might seem like a perfunctory topic in a literature review—everyone says they use Agile these days, and the word has been stretched to mean almost anything. But the choice actually matters here for a specific reason.

Traditional plan-driven approaches like Waterfall assume you can specify requirements upfront and then build to that specification (Sommerville, 2016). Agile, as articulated in the original manifesto (Beck et al., 2001) and operationalised through frameworks like Scrum (Schwaber & Sutherland, 2020), takes the opposite position: requirements evolve, and the development process should accommodate that evolution through short iterative cycles, working software at the end of each cycle, and continuous feedback.

For this particular project, Agile is not just a philosophical preference—it is a practical necessity. The researcher's own family runs the kind of contribution fund this system is designed for. That means requirements are not abstract. They come from real conversations: "Can the system handle it when someone pays three months in advance?" "What happens if we change the contribution amount midway through the year?" "I need a report that even Aunty Hauwa can understand." Those questions arise unpredictably, during family meetings or WhatsApp discussions, and the system needs to absorb them as they come rather than waiting for a formal change-request process.

The codebase itself follows the Model-View-Controller pattern—one of the oldest and most reliable structural patterns in software (Krasner & Pope, 1988; Gamma et al., 1994). Laravel enforces this by design: Eloquent models handle the database, controllers handle business logic and request processing, and Vue.js components (connected through Inertia.js) handle what the user sees. When you are a single developer building every layer of the stack, that separation is what keeps the project manageable.

### 2.3 Review of Related Work

Having laid out the theoretical foundations, this section turns to what has actually been built and studied. The goal is not just to catalogue existing work but to identify, concretely, where prior research and development stops—and where this project begins.

#### 2.3.1 Multi-Tenant SaaS Architectures in Practice

Multi-tenancy is not a new idea. Salesforce, Shopify, Slack—these are all multi-tenant platforms serving thousands of organisations from shared infrastructure while keeping each customer's data walled off from everyone else's. Fowler (2002) laid out the fundamental tension early on: you want to share resources for efficiency, but you also need strict boundaries for security and privacy. Multi-tenancy resolves that tension by making isolation a software concern rather than a hardware one.

Bezemer and Zaidman (2010) published one of the most cited academic analyses of how real SaaS applications handle this. They found, unsurprisingly, that shared-schema approaches demand the most careful engineering—particularly around data access patterns—but deliver the best cost efficiency per tenant. Chong et al. (2006) at Microsoft offered a practical decision framework: expected number of tenants, regulatory constraints, and customisation requirements should drive the isolation strategy.

What both of these studies have in common, though, is their focus on enterprise software. CRM systems. ERPs. Project management tools. Nobody has studied what happens when you apply multi-tenancy to informal financial management, where the "tenants" are families and the data at stake is personal—not corporate. Think about what a data leak means in each context. For a company, it means regulatory penalties, bad press, maybe a lawsuit. For a family fund platform, it means one family seeing another family's private financial records. The damage is relational, not regulatory—and arguably harder to repair.

#### 2.3.2 Access Control in Financial Applications

Banks and financial institutions have used RBAC for years, and for good reason. Regulatory compliance requires knowing exactly who authorised a transaction, who viewed an account, who generated a report. Ferraiolo et al. (2001) demonstrated that the NIST reference model could handle the access control needs of most organisations, and the financial sector adopted it because of the auditability it provides.

More recently, researchers have pushed toward Attribute-Based Access Control, or ABAC, where permissions factor in contextual attributes beyond just the user's role—things like time of day, device type, or geographic location (Hu et al., 2014). ABAC is undeniably more flexible than RBAC. It is also harder to reason about, harder to debug, and frankly overkill for a context where the governance structure is as simple as "three types of people, three sets of permissions."

What this project adds to the RBAC literature is modest but specific: it implements role-based access control independently within each tenant of a multi-tenant system. Most RBAC research assumes one organisation, one set of roles. In FamilyFunds, the same person could be an administrator in their Hussain family group and an ordinary member in their workplace savings club. Each family is its own access control domain. That per-tenant RBAC model is standard practice in large SaaS platforms, but it has never been described in the context of informal financial management.

#### 2.3.3 Digital Payment Integration in Sub-Saharan Africa

When M-Pesa launched in Kenya in 2007, it proved something that development economists had debated for years: mobile-phone-based financial services really could reach the unbanked at scale. Jack and Suri (2014) showed rigorously that M-Pesa improved household risk-sharing by slashing transaction costs—sending money across the country became cheap and near-instant instead of expensive and uncertain. Mbiti and Weil (2016) found an interesting side effect: as mobile money became available, people used informal savings groups less, because they now had individual alternatives.

Nigeria went a different direction. Rather than one dominant mobile money operator, the country developed an ecosystem of payment gateways—Paystack, Flutterwave, Interswitch—each plugging into the existing banking infrastructure (Aker & Mbiti, 2010; Paystack, 2024). Paystack in particular, acquired by Stripe in 2020, became the go-to option for developers because of its clean API and solid documentation for Naira-denominated transactions.

The consistent finding across this literature is that making financial transactions easier increases both participation and trust (Gomber et al., 2017). For a family fund, that translates directly: if a member can pay her contribution with a few taps on her phone and get an automatic receipt, she is more likely to pay on time than if she has to find cash, hand it to someone, and hope the record gets updated. And the platform can reconcile payments in real time rather than waiting for the financial secretary to open a notebook.

Nobody, though, has looked at payment gateway integration for this specific kind of group—one with multiple members, differentiated roles, tiered amounts, partial payments, and automated allocation logic. M-Pesa research is about person-to-person transfers. PiggyVest is individual savings. CreditClan is formal lending. The multi-party, role-governed integration that FamilyFunds requires does not fit neatly into any of those categories.

#### 2.3.4 Machine Learning for Credit and Payment Default Prediction

Credit scoring is probably the most thoroughly studied application of machine learning in finance. Thomas (2000) surveyed the field early on, documenting the dominance of logistic regression and discriminant analysis. Then ensemble methods arrived. Breiman (2001) introduced random forests. Chen and Guestrin (2016) built XGBoost. Both showed meaningful accuracy improvements on credit datasets, and the field has been racing through variations ever since.

Khandani et al. (2010) made an important contribution by demonstrating that transaction-level behavioural data—how someone actually uses financial products, not just their demographics—predicts credit risk better than traditional approaches. Lessmann et al. (2015) ran what amounts to a bake-off: 41 different classification algorithms on standard datasets. Random forests and gradient-boosted trees usually came out on top, though the gap between the best algorithms was often smaller than the gap between careful and sloppy feature engineering. Bellotti and Crook (2009) tried support vector machines and reached a similar conclusion—the choice of features matters more than the choice of model.

Hand (2009) questioned something more fundamental: whether the standard evaluation metric—the area under the ROC curve—even made sense for credit scoring. He argued, fairly persuasively, that AUC has incoherent mathematical properties that can mislead practitioners about actual model performance.

All of this research, though, operates in a universe of big data. Millions of loan records. Thousands of features. The family fund world is the opposite. Twenty members. Twelve months of history. A dataset you could print on two sheets of A4 paper. Nobody has tried to apply credit scoring techniques to data this small, in a context where the "cost" of a default is not a financial loss to a bank but a social rift in a family. Hastie et al. (2009) provide the theoretical grounding: with tiny samples, simpler models win because complex ones have nothing to generalise from.

#### 2.3.5 Natural Language Generation in Financial Contexts

Automated financial text generation is not new. The Associated Press has been using NLG systems to write corporate earnings stories since 2014—thousands of them, generated by Automated Insights' Wordsmith platform without a journalist typing a single sentence (Dale, 2020). Those earlier systems followed the classical NLG pipeline described by Reiter and Dale (2000): templates, rules, slot-filling, surface realisation. Effective for repetitive, narrowly scoped content. Terrible at handling anything the templates were not built for.

The Transformer architecture changed that completely. Vaswani et al. (2017) introduced the attention mechanism, Brown et al. (2020) scaled it up with GPT-3, Devlin et al. (2019) took a different architectural tack with BERT, and OpenAI (2023) pushed further with GPT-4. The upshot, for practical purposes, is that you no longer need to build domain-specific NLG pipelines. A general-purpose LLM can take structured data—any structured data—and turn it into fluent prose. Dale (2020) noted that the commercial NLG market was already pivoting hard toward neural approaches by 2020.

The challenge specific to financial reporting is accuracy. A template system cannot hallucinate because it only outputs what its rules generate. An LLM can and sometimes does. For family fund reports—where a wrong number could trigger an argument at the next family meeting—that risk has to be taken seriously. The approach FamilyFunds adopts is to supply all data in the prompt, so the model is performing data-to-text translation rather than drawing on its training data. That does not eliminate hallucination risk entirely, but it constrains it significantly.

Nobody has explored LLM-generated reports for this kind of audience. Corporate NLG assumes the reader knows what a P&L statement looks like. Robo-advisory platforms write for people who have at least opened an investment account. FamilyFunds needs to produce reports that a retired grandmother in Kaduna can read and understand just as well as a software engineer in Lagos. That is a different accessibility bar, and no published work has tried to clear it.

#### 2.3.6 Web Authentication and Passwordless Security

Passwords are terrible, and everyone knows it. Bonneau et al. (2012) wrote the definitive analysis: they evaluated every proposed password alternative against three dimensions—usability, deployability, and security—and found that nothing scored well on all three simultaneously. Passwords persisted not because they were good but because every replacement had a fatal drawback in at least one dimension.

WebAuthn might actually be the one that sticks. Standardised by the W3C in 2019 and updated to Level 2 in 2021 (W3C, 2021), it is built on the FIDO2 protocol (FIDO Alliance, 2022) and works through public-key cryptography. During registration, the user's device creates a key pair—private key stays on the device, often locked behind a fingerprint sensor or face scan, and the public key goes to the server. To log in, the server sends a challenge, the device signs it with the private key, and the server checks the signature. No password ever crosses the network. Nothing to phish, nothing to leak in a database breach.

Lyastani et al. (2020) ran one of the first proper usability studies and found that users thought the process was faster and more convenient than passwords—once they understood it. The problem was the "once they understood it" part. Many participants had no idea what a "passkey" was or how it related to the fingerprint sensor on their phone. Device support was another hurdle: not every phone or laptop has the right hardware.

FamilyFunds handles this pragmatically. WebAuthn is there for members whose devices support it—faster login, stronger security. Everyone else gets traditional email-and-password login with TOTP-based two-factor authentication as a second layer. The idea, consistent with what Ometov et al. (2018) recommended, is to let the user's device capabilities determine the authentication flow rather than forcing everyone through the same gate.

#### 2.3.7 Single Page Application Frameworks and Server-Driven Architectures

The move from traditional multi-page web applications—where every click triggers a full page reload—to single page applications was one of the biggest shifts in web development over the past decade. Mikowski and Powell (2013) made the early case: move rendering to the client, and the application feels faster, more responsive, more like a native app. React, Vue.js, and Angular all run with this idea, each with a different philosophy about how to do it.

Vue.js (You, 2024), the framework this project uses, is the most approachable of the three. Its reactivity model is intuitive rather than ceremonial, and it scales from a small widget to a large application without drowning you in boilerplate—which matters a lot when one person is building the entire frontend.

Inertia.js (Reinink, 2024) sits on top of Vue and does something clever: it gives you the feel of a SPA—no full page reloads, dynamic content updates—without requiring you to build and maintain a separate API. The server stays in charge of routing and data loading, responding with JSON instead of HTML. The Vue adapter on the client swaps components based on what comes back. The result is what Reinink calls a "server-driven SPA."

Why does this matter for FamilyFunds? Because it means all of Laravel's existing machinery—middleware, authentication, authorisation, validation, route definitions—works exactly the way it would in a traditional server-rendered application. There is no separate set of API routes to maintain, no token-based authentication to configure, no validation logic to duplicate between backend and frontend. Laravel Wayfinder takes it a step further by auto-generating TypeScript functions from the server-side route definitions, so the frontend gets type safety without anyone having to manually keep things in sync.

### 2.4 Gaps in Existing Research

The literature reviewed above is extensive within each domain. Multi-tenancy has been thoroughly studied for enterprise software. RBAC has been implemented across the financial sector. Mobile money has reshaped African economies. Credit scoring has been refined by two decades of machine learning research. NLG has gone from templates to neural models. WebAuthn is maturing as a password replacement. SPA frameworks have become standard.

But none of these threads cross into the territory this project occupies. When you look at where the research ends, the gaps are specific and concrete.

**No multi-tenant architecture for informal family finance.** Every study of multi-tenancy assumes commercial tenants—companies paying for SaaS subscriptions. Nobody has applied the pattern to informal groups where the consequences of a data boundary failure are not regulatory fines but destroyed family relationships. FamilyFunds implements shared-schema multi-tenancy with application-level isolation enforced through middleware and policies, tailored to a context where trust is personal rather than contractual.

**RBAC has never been formalised for family fund governance.** Access control research in finance focuses on banks and regulated institutions. The informal, relationship-based governance of family funds—where "Uncle Ahmed handles the money" is the entire access control policy—has never been translated into a technical enforcement mechanism. This project does that with a three-tier role model inside each family tenant.

**Payment integration for multi-party informal groups is uncharted.** The fintech literature covers individual transfers, individual savings, and formal lending. Nobody has studied what it takes to integrate a payment gateway into a context with multiple members, tiered amounts, partial payments, oldest-balance-first allocation, and role-governed recording. The closest analogue does not exist.

**Machine learning has not been tested on datasets this small or this informal.** Credit scoring research runs on millions of records from banks. A family fund generates hundreds of records at most, in a context where defaults are social events rather than financial ones. Whether ML classification techniques have anything useful to say at that scale is genuinely an open question.

**LLM-generated reports for non-expert family audiences are unexplored.** NLG in finance targets analysts, investors, and professionals. Using LLMs to produce reports that a retired grandmother can read and act on—that is a different problem with different constraints, and nobody has published on it.

**Finally, and most importantly, the integration itself is novel.** Each of these capabilities exists somewhere. Multi-tenancy powers Salesforce. RBAC runs in every bank. Paystack processes millions of transactions. Credit scoring models are everywhere. LLMs generate text on demand. WebAuthn is shipping in browsers. But no platform puts them all together for informal family-level financial management. The combination, applied to this specific problem, is what makes this project's contribution distinct.

### 2.5 Summary of the Literature Review

This chapter covered a lot of ground. It started with the deep roots of collective finance in Nigeria—*ajo*, *esusu*, *adashi*, and the less-studied family purse—and traced how these practices persist despite the rise of digital banking, largely because no digital tool actually serves them. It then set out the theoretical underpinnings of the technologies this project brings together: multi-tenant architecture, RBAC, FIFO-based payment allocation, supervised classification, LLM-powered text generation, and Agile development practices.

The review of related work spanned seven domains and showed the same thing in each one: plenty of research, plenty of working systems, but all aimed at contexts fundamentally different from informal family fund management. Enterprise SaaS tenants are not family groups. Banking RBAC serves regulated institutions, not kinship-based governance. M-Pesa and Paystack transformed individual payments but left group-based finance untouched. Credit scoring works on massive datasets that family funds cannot produce. NLG has automated corporate reporting but never targeted audiences with no financial literacy. WebAuthn is replacing passwords, but doing so inclusively—in a way that accommodates members whose devices lack biometric hardware—requires careful layering. And server-driven SPAs through Inertia.js provide the architectural foundation that makes all of this buildable by a single developer without maintaining a separate API.

The six gaps identified—in multi-tenancy, access control, payment integration, machine learning, natural language generation, and their integration—collectively define what this project contributes. It is not a theoretical advance. It is an engineering one: taking established technologies and applying them, together, to a real problem that affects millions of Nigerian families and has no existing solution.

The next chapter translates this theoretical and empirical groundwork into a system design—specifying requirements, architecture, database structure, and the development methodology that will guide implementation.
