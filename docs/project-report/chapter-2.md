# CHAPTER TWO

## LITERATURE REVIEW

This chapter presents a comprehensive review of the literature relevant to the design and implementation of an AI-enhanced multi-tenant family fund management system. The review is organised into five sections: historical perspectives on family fund management systems, the theoretical framework underpinning the system design, a detailed review of related work, an identification of gaps in existing research, and a summary that establishes how this project addresses those gaps. In total, this chapter draws upon over fifty scholarly sources spanning informal finance, software architecture, access control, financial technology, machine learning, natural language generation, and web authentication.

### 2.1 Historical Perspectives of Family Fund Management Systems

#### 2.1.1 Origins of Informal Collective Finance in Africa

The practice of pooling financial resources among groups of individuals bound by social, familial, or occupational ties is deeply rooted in the cultural and economic history of Sub-Saharan Africa. These informal financial arrangements predate the establishment of formal banking institutions on the continent and have been documented across virtually every region and ethnic group (Bouman, 1995; Ardener, 1964). In her seminal anthropological study, Ardener (1964) provided one of the earliest systematic accounts of rotating savings and credit associations (ROSCAs) across multiple cultures, establishing the foundational taxonomy that scholars continue to reference. She observed that these associations function not merely as economic instruments but as social institutions that reinforce trust, reciprocity, and communal obligation.

In Nigeria, informal savings mechanisms have assumed various culturally specific forms. The *ajo* system, prevalent among the Yoruba of southwestern Nigeria, involves a group of individuals who contribute fixed amounts at regular intervals, with each member receiving the total pool in rotation (Bascom, 1952). The *esusu*, also Yoruba in origin but widely practised across the country, operates similarly but with a more formalised structure and, in some variants, an appointed treasurer who manages disbursements (Akanji, 2006). In northern Nigeria, the *adashi* system serves a comparable function among Hausa-speaking communities, where members contribute to a common pool that is distributed on a rotating basis or retained for collective use (Iganiga & Asemota, 2008). Across francophone West and Central Africa, the analogous institution is the *tontine*, named after the 17th-century Italian banker Lorenzo de Tonti, which has evolved into a widespread mechanism for cooperative savings and micro-lending (Ardener & Burman, 1995).

These systems share common structural features: voluntary membership, periodic fixed contributions, a predetermined allocation sequence or collective retention, and governance by social norms rather than legal contracts (Bouman, 1995). Their persistence alongside the growth of formal banking is attributable not to the absence of alternatives, but to the unique advantages they offer—accessibility, flexibility, minimal transaction costs, and the social reinforcement of financial discipline (Aryeetey, 2008; Oloyede, 2008). Gugerty (2007) demonstrated empirically that participation in ROSCAs in Kenya served as a commitment device, helping members overcome self-control problems in saving—a finding that resonates with the Nigerian context where participation in *ajo* or family contribution funds is often motivated by the desire for imposed financial discipline.

#### 2.1.2 The Family Purse: A Distinct Non-Rotating Collective Fund

While ROSCAs such as *ajo*, *esusu*, and *adashi* have received substantial scholarly attention, a less-documented but equally significant variant of collective finance exists: the **family contribution fund**, or family purse. Unlike rotating associations where pooled funds cycle through members in sequence, the family purse operates as a **non-rotating, centrally managed treasury**. Members—typically drawn from an extended family or kinship network—contribute fixed or tiered monthly amounts into a common pool that is retained collectively and disbursed as needed for shared expenses: funerals, medical emergencies, educational support, celebrations, and property maintenance (Nnama-Okechukwu & Okoye, 2019).

The family purse model differs from ROSCAs in several important respects. First, there is no rotation—members do not take turns receiving the pooled funds. Second, contributions may be tiered based on the member's economic capacity (e.g., employed members contributing more than students or unemployed members), introducing a dimension of equity-based differentiation absent from standard ROSCAs. Third, the management responsibility typically falls on a single designated individual—an elder or elected financial secretary—who maintains records, collects payments, authorises expenditures, and reports to the family (Alabi et al., 2019). This centralisation creates both efficiency and vulnerability: while it reduces coordination costs, it places a disproportionate burden of trust on one individual and introduces risks of mismanagement, whether intentional or inadvertent.

The prevalence of family contribution funds in Nigeria is difficult to quantify precisely because they operate informally and are not captured in conventional financial surveys. However, the World Bank Global Findex Database reported that approximately 55% of Nigerian adults who save do so through informal mechanisms rather than formal financial institutions (Demirgüç-Kunt et al., 2022), and EFInA (2023) estimated that over 36 million Nigerian adults rely exclusively on informal financial groups. While these figures encompass all forms of informal saving, they strongly suggest that family-level collective funds constitute a significant subset, given the centrality of extended family structures in Nigerian social organisation (Nnama-Okechukwu & Okoye, 2019).

#### 2.1.3 Management Challenges: From Notebooks to Messaging Platforms

The management of family contribution funds has historically relied on rudimentary record-keeping methods. In the pre-digital era, the appointed financial secretary would maintain handwritten ledgers or notebooks recording each member's contributions, outstanding balances, and expenses (Adeyemo & Bamire, 2005). These physical records, while functional at small scale, are inherently vulnerable to loss, damage, arithmetic errors, and intentional manipulation. The absence of an independent audit trail means that disputes over payments—whether a member has paid, how much they paid, and for which period—are resolved through memory and social negotiation rather than verifiable documentation.

The widespread adoption of mobile telephony and messaging platforms in Nigeria has introduced a new layer to this management challenge. Increasingly, family fund administrators use WhatsApp groups or similar platforms to announce contribution due dates, confirm receipt of payments, and share financial summaries (Ogunleye & Adewale, 2021). While this represents an improvement in communication reach—particularly for geographically dispersed families—it does not constitute a management system. Messages are ephemeral, unsearchable in any structured sense, and lack computational capabilities for balance tracking, payment allocation, or report generation. Furthermore, the informality of messaging-based management creates ambiguity: a WhatsApp message confirming receipt of "20k from Brother Musa" does not specify whether this is a full or partial payment, which contribution period it applies to, or what the remaining balance is.

Osondu et al. (2015) documented similar challenges among cooperative societies in southeastern Nigeria, noting that manual record-keeping was the single greatest source of disputes and eventual dissolution of savings groups. Their findings align with the broader literature on informal financial institutions, which consistently identifies record-keeping inadequacy as a primary threat to the sustainability of collective savings arrangements (Anderson & Baland, 2002; Aryeetey, 2008).

#### 2.1.4 The Digital Transformation of Cooperative Finance

The broader landscape of financial technology (fintech) in Africa has undergone rapid transformation since the introduction of M-Pesa in Kenya in 2007, which demonstrated the viability of mobile-phone-based financial services for unbanked and underbanked populations (Mbiti & Weil, 2016). This innovation catalysed a wave of fintech development across the continent, including Nigeria, where platforms such as Paystack, Flutterwave, and Interswitch have built payment infrastructure that enables digital transactions at scale (Adegbite & Machethe, 2020; Paystack, 2024).

However, the digitisation of cooperative and group-based finance has lagged behind individual financial services. Platforms such as PiggyVest and Cowrywise have achieved significant adoption in Nigeria for individual savings and investment, offering features such as automated savings, fixed-return investments, and peer-to-peer savings challenges (PiggyVest, 2024; Cowrywise, 2024). These platforms are designed for individual users managing their personal finances and do not support the multi-group, role-governed, collectively managed financial operations that characterise family funds.

On the cooperative management side, platforms such as CreditClan and Lendsqr provide digital infrastructure for formal lending cooperatives, including loan management, credit scoring, and regulatory reporting (CreditClan, 2024). These solutions, while sophisticated, target registered cooperative societies with regulatory compliance requirements under the Companies and Allied Matters Act (CAMA) and Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) guidelines—requirements that are entirely irrelevant to informal family contribution groups.

A small number of mobile applications have emerged targeting group savings, such as Esusu (a US-based platform that draws on the West African concept but targets formal rent reporting for credit building) and various M-Pesa-integrated group savings tools in East Africa (Mbiti & Weil, 2016). However, none of these platforms combine the specific feature set required for family fund management: multi-tenant data isolation, tiered contributions based on member categories, role-based access control with differentiated administrative and member permissions, partial payment tracking with automated allocation, and integrated financial reporting—let alone the predictive analytics and intelligent narrative reporting capabilities proposed in this project.

#### 2.1.5 The Imperative for Purpose-Built Solutions

The historical review presented in this section establishes several key observations. First, collective financial management within families and kinship networks is a deeply rooted, culturally significant, and economically consequential practice across Nigeria and Sub-Saharan Africa. Second, the family purse model—a non-rotating, centrally managed treasury—is distinct from the better-documented ROSCA model and presents unique management challenges related to tiered contributions, partial payments, and centralised administration. Third, while the broader fintech landscape has advanced considerably, no existing digital platform specifically addresses the compound requirements of family-level fund management. This gap—between the scale and importance of the practice and the absence of purpose-built digital tools—provides the historical and contextual justification for the system proposed in this project.
